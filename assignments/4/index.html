Below we have made a visualization of a dataset consisting of outlines of hands. The dataset has 40 hands with 112 dimensions, that is 56 points with an x- and y-coordinate. In the left graph you can see the outline of a one of the hands. This is pretty cool in itself, but want we want to showcase if is the utility of Principal Component Analysis (PCA).
PCA is a statistical method which reduces a dataset to fewer dimensions and orders the dimensions from most significant to least significant. For our visualization we have reduced the number of dimensions from 112 to 40. As we know the two first dimensions are the most significant we can plot these and expect it to be meaningful. This is the rightmost plot.
In this plot you can hover over the individual points and see the values of the pair and which hand number it is. If you click a point the matching hand will be drawn in the left plot. Now the interesting thing is what these coordinates says about the hand. Remember that we have reduced a hand consisting of 112 coordinates to just 2 coordinates.
If you select the rightmost points the fingers of the corresponding hands are pretty close to each other and are pointing straight left. Conversely the hands on the left side of the graph are spread. If we look at the lone point in the bottom right, its lower fingers are tight together while the other fingers are far apart. The hand at the top of the graph have the top right fingers close together while the bottom two are spread. In the middle of the graph the fingers are in similar positions.
So to sum it up. We have used PCA to reduce our dataset to just two points per hands, these coordinates have no intrinsic meaning and yet when we plot the initial dataset we see that we can make a good guess at the look of the matching hands based on the value of our two principal components.
